Diffusion Models

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)

March 15, 2026 · Based on Ho, Jain & Abbeel (NeurIPS 2020)

Introduction

Diffusion models belong to a family called Generative Models. The shared goal across all generative models is to learn the underlying distribution of real data — such as images — so that we can sample new, realistic data points from that distribution. However, each approach takes a fundamentally different mathematical route.

GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks) use two competing neural networks. VAEs (Variational Autoencoders) encode data into a latent space and decode back. Flow-based models use invertible transformations for exact likelihood computation. And Diffusion Models — the focus here — take a unique approach: gradually adding noise to data, then learning to reverse that process.

Today, diffusion models power some of the most impressive AI applications: image-to-image translation, text-to-image generation (Stable Diffusion, DALL-E), and even image-to-video generation.

The Forward Diffusion Process

The forward process starts with a real data sample x₀ drawn from the true data distribution. We then gradually add small amounts of Gaussian noise over T timesteps. At each step t, we define a conditional distribution:

q(xₜ | xₜ₋₁) = N(xₜ; √(1-βₜ) · xₜ₋₁, βₜI)

The noise level is controlled by a variance schedule {βₜ ∈ (0,1)}. As t increases, the original data loses its features — first the fine textures disappear, then the edges, then the shapes — until eventually xₜ becomes indistinguishable from pure Gaussian noise.

The Reparameterization Trick

A critical efficiency insight: we don't need to simulate every step sequentially. Using the reparameterization trick, we can jump directly to any timestep t:

xₜ = √(ᾱₜ) · x₀ + √(1 - ᾱₜ) · ε

where ᾱₜ is the cumulative product of αᵢ = 1 - βᵢ, and ε is standard Gaussian noise. This lets us efficiently create training samples at any noise level.

Training Objective

The purpose of the forward process is to train a neural network to predict the noise inside an image. We take a clean image, add noise to create xₜ, feed it into the network, and the network predicts the noise that was injected. The loss is simply Mean Squared Error between predicted and actual noise.

The Reverse Diffusion Process

The reverse process is where image generation actually happens. Starting from pure noise, we gradually reconstruct a realistic image. We train a neural network pθ to approximate the reverse conditional probabilities:

pθ(xₜ₋₁ | xₜ) = N(xₜ₋₁; μθ(xₜ, t), Σθ(xₜ, t))

In practice: feed a noisy image xₜ into the pretrained network, get the predicted noise, compute the predicted clean image, add back a controlled amount of noise, and obtain xₜ₋₁. Repeat until we reach x₀.

Why Diffusion Models Avoid Blurry Results

This is one of the most important questions. MSE loss typically produces blurry results in autoencoders and super-resolution networks. Yet diffusion models, using the same MSE loss, produce sharp images. Why?

Two Key Insights

  1. Predicting noise forces the model to learn high-frequency details. Gaussian noise is pure high-frequency content. The model must distinguish between random noise and real texture details like fur, skin pores, or fabric patterns. This prevents the model from ignoring fine details.
  2. Multiple timesteps decompose uncertainty. At early stages (t ≈ 999), all fine details are destroyed — the model only needs to decide the large-scale structure (where the cat is, what direction it faces). At late stages (t ≈ 1), the structure is already fixed — the model only needs to add fine texture details. Each step has a well-defined, low-ambiguity task.

Together, these two components prevent the "one-to-many" averaging problem that causes blurriness in direct prediction architectures.

Noise Schedule & Efficient Sampling

Linear vs. Cosine Schedule

The original DDPM used a linear schedule — increasing β linearly. The flaw: the signal drops to near zero very quickly (around t = 0.4), meaning the last 40% of training steps are wasted on pure noise. The cosine schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal) uses a cosine function to smooth the curve, ensuring a more gradual transition and better utilization of all timesteps.

Resolution Matters

Higher-resolution images have more redundancy. The same noise injection heavily corrupts a 64×64 image but barely affects a 1024×1024 image. This means noise schedules need to be adjusted for different resolutions.

Faster Sampling: DDIM & Euler

DDIM (Denoising Diffusion Implicit Models) transforms the stochastic process into a deterministic mapping — the same initial noise always produces the same image. It proved we can generate high-quality images in just 50 steps instead of 1000. Euler schedulers have become the default for state-of-the-art models, offering yet more efficient sampling.

Architecture Evolution: LDM → DiT

Latent Diffusion Models (LDM)

Instead of running diffusion on raw pixels, LDM operates in a compressed latent space using a VQ-GAN encoder-decoder. This reduces the computational complexity by a factor of ~8, making high-resolution generation feasible on consumer GPUs. The U-Net backbone is augmented with cross-attention mechanisms to incorporate text conditioning from CLIP.

Diffusion Transformer (DiT)

DiT replaces the U-Net entirely with a standard Vision Transformer (ViT). The motivation: U-Nets are architecturally complex with downsampling, upsampling, skip connections, and convolutional biases. Transformers offer a simpler, more scalable architecture. Key innovations include:

  • Patchify: Divides the latent input into non-overlapping patches, creating token sequences for the transformer.
  • AdaLN-Zero: Uses adaptive Layer Normalization to inject timestep and label conditioning, avoiding the need for separate cross-attention layers.
  • Zero initialization: Output MLP weights start at zero, preventing random conditioning signals from disrupting early training.

MultiModal-DiT (MM-DiT)

The architecture behind Stable Diffusion 3.5, MM-DiT processes image and text in separate streams with Joint Attention — allowing image tokens and text tokens to attend to each other directly, while maintaining separate processing paths for everything else.

Practical Extensions

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)

Full fine-tuning of diffusion models is expensive (2-4GB per style). LoRA exploits the insight that weight updates have a low intrinsic rank — the necessary changes can be represented by two small matrices. This enables efficient fine-tuning with minimal additional parameters.

ControlNet

A plug-and-play module that adds structural control (edge maps, depth maps, pose skeletons) to text-to-image generation by creating a trainable copy of the encoder with zero convolutions.

Conclusion

The evolution of Diffusion Models represents a fundamental shift in generative AI. Unlike GANs, they offer stable training with a simple MSE objective. The progression from pixel-space DDPM to latent-space LDM to transformer-based DiT has made high-quality generation increasingly accessible and scalable. Combined with conditioning mechanisms like cross-attention, LoRA for efficient fine-tuning, and ControlNet for spatial control, diffusion models have become the backbone of modern generative AI.
