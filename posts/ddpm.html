Diffusion Models

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM)

March 15, 2026 · Based on Ho, Jain & Abbeel (NeurIPS 2020)

What Are Diffusion Models?

Diffusion models are a type of model that learns to create new images. The idea is simple: take a clean image, slowly add noise until it becomes pure static, then train a network to undo that noise step by step.

Other methods exist too — GANs use two networks that compete, VAEs compress and rebuild images, and Flow models use reversible math. Diffusion models stand out because they are stable to train and produce sharp results.

They power tools like Stable Diffusion and DALL-E for turning text into images, and even for making videos.

Adding Noise (Forward Process)

Start with a clean image x₀. At each step t, add a small amount of random noise. After enough steps (usually 1000), the image becomes pure noise — you can't tell what it used to be.

q(xₜ | xₜ₋₁) = N(xₜ; √(1-βₜ) · xₜ₋₁, βₜI)

A schedule {βₜ} controls how much noise is added at each step. Early steps add little noise, later steps add more.

Jumping to Any Step

We don't need to add noise one step at a time. Using a math trick, we can jump straight to any noise level:

xₜ = √(ᾱₜ) · x₀ + √(1 - ᾱₜ) · ε

This makes training much faster since we can pick any random step to practice on.

What the Network Learns

The network takes a noisy image and tries to guess what noise was added. The training loss is just the difference (MSE) between the real noise and the predicted noise.

Removing Noise (Reverse Process)

To create a new image, start from pure noise and remove it step by step. At each step the network predicts the noise, we subtract some of it, and repeat until we get a clean image.

pθ(xₜ₋₁ | xₜ) = N(xₜ₋₁; μθ(xₜ, t), Σθ(xₜ, t))

Why the Results Are Sharp, Not Blurry

MSE loss usually makes blurry images. But diffusion models avoid this for two reasons:

  1. Predicting noise means learning details. Noise is all high-frequency content, so the network must learn to tell apart real textures (like fur or skin) from random noise.
  2. Each step handles one level of detail. Early steps decide the big picture (shape, layout). Late steps add fine details (texture, edges). No single step has too much to figure out.

Noise Schedules and Faster Sampling

Linear vs. Cosine

The original DDPM used a linear schedule that added noise too fast — the last 40% of steps were wasted. A cosine schedule spreads the noise more evenly so every step is useful.

Image Size Matters

The same amount of noise damages a small image (64×64) much more than a large one (1024×1024). So the schedule needs adjusting for different sizes.

Fewer Steps with DDIM and Euler

DDIM showed that you can skip most steps and still get good images — 50 steps instead of 1000. Euler schedulers are now common and even faster.

From U-Net to Transformers

Latent Diffusion (LDM)

Instead of working on full-size images, LDM first compresses them into a smaller space using an encoder. Diffusion runs in this smaller space, which is about 8× cheaper. A U-Net does the denoising, and text input from CLIP is mixed in through cross-attention.

Diffusion Transformer (DiT)

DiT replaces the U-Net with a Vision Transformer. Transformers are simpler and scale better. Key ideas:

  • Patchify: Cut the image into patches and treat them as tokens.
  • AdaLN-Zero: Feed the timestep and label info through layer normalization instead of extra attention layers.
  • Zero init: Start output weights at zero so the model trains smoothly from the start.

MM-DiT

Used in Stable Diffusion 3.5. It runs image and text in separate paths but lets them look at each other through joint attention.

Useful Add-ons

LoRA

Fine-tuning a whole diffusion model is expensive. LoRA adds just two small matrices to each layer, making fine-tuning much cheaper while keeping quality high.

ControlNet

Lets you control the output using things like edge maps, depth maps, or body poses. It copies part of the model and trains the copy to follow these guides.

Summary

Diffusion models add noise then learn to remove it. They train stably, produce sharp images, and have grown from simple DDPM to fast transformer-based systems like DiT. With tools like LoRA and ControlNet, they are now easy to fine-tune and control.
