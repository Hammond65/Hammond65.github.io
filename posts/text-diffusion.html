Text-Guided Image Generation with Prompt-to-Prompt Editing

February 15, 2026 · Hertz et al. (ICLR 2023)

How Does Text Control Image Generation?

Diffusion models remove noise step by step to create images. But how does the model know what to draw? This post explains how text guides the process — from turning words into numbers, to how each pixel decides which words matter to it.

CLIP: Turning Words into Numbers

The Problem

Networks only understand numbers. We need to turn "A cute cat on grass" into numbers that carry visual meaning. Simple numbering (cat=1, dog=2) doesn't work because it makes "cat" and "dog" just as different as "cat" and "airplane."

How CLIP Works

CLIP was trained on 400 million image-text pairs. It has two parts:

  • Image encoder: turns an image into a vector of numbers
  • Text encoder: turns a sentence into a vector of numbers

During training, matching image-text pairs are pushed close together, and non-matching pairs are pushed apart. After training, similar concepts end up near each other in this number space.

What Do We Get?

For diffusion models, we only use the text encoder. It takes a sentence, breaks it into up to 77 words, and turns each into a 768-number vector. The words interact with each other through the network, so "cute" learns it describes "cat" and not "grass." The output is a 77×768 table of numbers.

Cross-Attention: How Pixels Read Text

Cross-attention lets each pixel ask: "Which words are important to me?"

The Steps

  1. Query (Q): Each pixel creates a question — "What do I need?"
  2. Key (K): Each word creates a label — "This is what I am"
  3. Value (V): Each word creates content — "This is the information I carry"

The pixel's query is compared with every word's key to find which words matter most. Then the pixel collects information from those words.

For example, a pixel in the center of a cat image might get: 82% of "cat" information, 11% of "grass," 4% of "cute," and barely anything from "A" or "on."

Multiple Attention Heads

One attention head learns one kind of link between text and image. But there are many kinds — which object goes where, what color things are, how objects relate to each other. So the model uses 8 heads in parallel, each learning a different type of connection.

DAAM: Seeing What the Model Sees

DAAM collects all the attention maps from every step and layer, then combines them into one heatmap per word. This shows which parts of the image each word influenced.

Key findings:

  • "Dog" lights up over the dog area; "book" lights up over the book
  • Describing words like "fluffy" overlap with the thing they describe
  • Action words like "reading" light up between the objects involved
  • Early steps show rough layout; later steps show fine details

Prompt-to-Prompt: Editing with Just Words

Normally, changing even one word in your prompt gives you a completely different image. Prompt-to-Prompt fixes this by reusing the attention maps from the original image.

Word Swap

Generate "a cat on a bench" and save the attention maps. Then change to "a dog on a bench" but force the model to keep the same layout. Result: a dog appears in the exact same pose and position as the cat.

Changing Strength

You can also make a word stronger or weaker. For "a fluffy cat," turning up the weight of "fluffy" makes the cat fluffier. This works because each word always sits at the same position in the attention map.

Summary

CLIP turns words into meaningful numbers. Cross-attention lets each pixel pick the words that matter to it. DAAM lets us see this happening. And Prompt-to-Prompt shows we can edit images just by changing words, because attention maps control where things appear.
