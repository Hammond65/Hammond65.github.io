MimicMotion: High-Quality Human Motion Video Generation with Confidence-Aware Pose Guidance

March 5, 2026 · Zhang et al. (ICML 2025)

What is Pose-Guided Video Generation?

Pose-guided video generation takes a static reference image and makes it move according to pose sequences extracted from a driving video. The concept is straightforward: extract a person's body pose (position and movements) from a video, then use that pose information to guide the reference image into motion.

The four main goals are: Motion Transfer (copy movements from a video to a still photo), Identity Preservation (keep the original appearance and clothing), Realistic Animation (smooth, natural-looking motion), and Flexible Control (no complex 3D modeling or motion capture needed).

Background: Key Components

DWPose: Whole-Body Pose Estimation

Before guiding video generation with poses, we need to extract them accurately. DWPose (ICCV 2023) detects 133 whole-body keypoints: 17 body, 6 feet, 68 face, and 42 hand points. It solves the problem of existing models like OpenPose being either slow or inaccurate on fine-grained details. Using knowledge distillation, a lightweight "student" model learns to outperform a heavy "teacher" model.

AnimateAnyone: The Baseline

AnimateAnyone is a fundamental baseline for pose-guided generation built on Stable Diffusion. Its architecture has three components: a Denoising UNet (with 2D convolution, spatial, cross, and temporal attention), a ReferenceNet (identical framework without temporal layers), and a Pose Guider. Training uses two stages — spatial (single frame) then temporal (video sequence). Despite good results, it still shows flickering and background instability.

Stable Video Diffusion (SVD)

SVD is an image-to-video generation model built on Stable Diffusion v2.1, modified with temporal convolution and attention layers inserted after every spatial layer (adding 656M new parameters, totaling 1.521 billion). It follows a three-stage training pipeline: (I) image pretraining, (II) video pretraining on 152M filtered video clips, and (III) high-quality fine-tuning. SVD generates short 3-4 second videos with realistic motion, but without explicit pose control.

MimicMotion's Three Contributions

1. Progressive Latent Fusion for Long Videos

The model is trained on 16-frame clips, limiting inference to short videos. Inspired by MultiDiffusion (a training-free panorama generation approach), MimicMotion breaks long videos into smaller overlapping segments and fuses them during denoising in latent space.

The innovation over simple equal-weight blending: adaptive weighting based on frame position. Frames at the center of a segment have more context — they can see both past and future frames through temporal attention. Edge frames have only one-sided context. The weighting follows a bell curve distribution, giving center frames higher weight.

The "bidirectional context" logic: imagine your video is a loaf of sliced bread. Spatial attention looks within one slice (checking consistency within a single frame). Temporal attention sticks a skewer through the loaf — looking at the same pixel across all frames. Center frames can look both backwards and forwards; edge frames can only look in one direction.

2. Confidence-Aware Pose Guidance

DWPose outputs keypoint locations along with confidence levels. The problem: to the video generation model, a keypoint with 0.31 confidence looks identical to one with 0.99 confidence — both are rendered as the same bright line on a black background.

Standard approaches use binary thresholding (above threshold = draw, below = don't). MimicMotion instead uses confidence as a continuous weight fed into the network:

  • High Confidence (0.99): "You MUST put a hand here"
  • Marginal Confidence (0.35): "Maybe put a hand here, but if it looks weird, ignore it"

This prevents false-positive keypoints from creating artifacts in the generated video.

3. Hand Region Enhancement

Hands occupy only ~10% of image pixels but are crucial for realistic video. Standard loss computation means hands contribute only ~10% to the total loss signal, while body/background dominates at ~90%. The model prioritizes body and neglects hand details.

MimicMotion's solution: when all hand keypoints exceed the confidence threshold, multiply the loss weight for hand regions by 10×. This forces the model to pay more attention to hand quality during training.

Implementation Details

  • Dataset: 4,436 human dancing videos from the internet (avg. 20.1 seconds)
  • Foundation: SVD pretrained weights; PoseNet trained from scratch
  • Training: 8× A100 GPUs, 20 epochs, batch size 8, 16 frames per clip, learning rate 10⁻⁵

Conclusion

MimicMotion effectively solves the jitter and artifact problems of previous pose-guided models. By combining Stable Video Diffusion with confidence-aware mechanisms and progressive latent fusion, it sets a new state-of-the-art for generating long, smooth, and controllable human motion videos. The practical results demonstrate good temporal stability, minimizing the flickering artifacts commonly seen in earlier approaches.
