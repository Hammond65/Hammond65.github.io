Face Recognition

ArcFace: Additive Angular Margin Loss for Deep Face Recognition

March 10, 2026 · Deng et al. (CVPR 2019)

The Problem: Faces Change During Video Generation

When generating videos from a single photo (using tools like MimicMotion), the face often drifts — frame 1 looks right, but by frame 20 the person looks different. To fix this, we need a way to turn a face into numbers and measure how similar two faces are. ArcFace does both.

How Face Recognition Works

Short History

  • Eigenfaces (1991): Used PCA to shrink face images. Simple but breaks with different lighting or angles.
  • DeepFace (2014): A deep network trained on 4M faces. Reached near-human accuracy.
  • FaceNet (2015): Trained with triplet loss to put similar faces close together in number space.

The Steps

  1. Find the face in the image
  2. Align it using 5 landmark points (eyes, nose, mouth corners)
  3. Extract features: a network turns the face into a vector of 512 numbers — this is the embedding
  4. Compare: measure the angle between two embeddings to decide if they're the same person

What Is a Face Embedding?

A list of 512 numbers that captures what makes a face unique — things like face shape, eye spacing, nose size. It ignores stuff that changes like expression, lighting, or camera angle.

Why Older Loss Functions Don't Work Well

Triplet loss (used by FaceNet) needs carefully chosen training groups of three images, which is slow and tricky. SoftMax is simpler but doesn't push different people's embeddings far enough apart — it only needs the correct class to win by a tiny bit.

Also, regular dot-product scoring mixes up two things: how long the vector is and which direction it points. We only care about the direction.

What ArcFace Does Differently

Step 1: Normalize Everything

Force all weight vectors and feature vectors to length 1. Now the score is just the cosine of the angle between them — only direction matters.

Step 2: Add an Angular Margin

During training, ArcFace makes the task harder on purpose. It adds an extra angle m to the correct class:

L = -log(es·cos(θy + m) / (es·cos(θy + m) + Σ es·cos(θj)))

This means the network must align each face more tightly with its correct class. Like training to score 80% instead of just barely passing — it makes the model much more reliable.

Training Details

  • Input: 112×112 aligned face images
  • Network: ResNet-100 → 512-d output
  • Data: MS1MV2 with 85,742 people

Summary

ArcFace turns faces into 512-number vectors and uses an angular margin to make sure different people are far apart in that space. It's simple, works well, and gives us a reliable way to check if a generated face still looks like the original person.
