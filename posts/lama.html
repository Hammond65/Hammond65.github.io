Image Inpainting

LaMa: Resolution-Robust Large Mask Inpainting with Fourier Convolutions

February 28, 2026 · Suvorov et al. (WACV 2022)

What Is Inpainting?

Inpainting means filling in missing parts of an image. If you erase someone from a photo, the model needs to fill that hole with something that looks natural. Small holes are easy — just look at nearby pixels. But when the hole is large, the model needs to understand the whole scene to fill it in correctly.

Normal convolution layers only look at a small area at a time. Even with many layers stacked together, they struggle to see the big picture. LaMa solves this.

Three Main Ideas

1. Fast Fourier Convolutions (FFC)

LaMa replaces normal convolutions with FFC blocks. Each FFC block has two paths:

  • Local path: Works like a normal convolution — looks at a small area around each pixel.
  • Global path: Uses the Fourier Transform to look at the entire image at once. It converts the image into frequency patterns, processes them, then converts back.

Why Does the Frequency Trick Work?

In pixel space, each value only describes one spot. In frequency space, each value carries information from the whole image. So even one operation in frequency space already "sees" everything.

Why It Scales to Any Size

A normal 3×3 convolution covers less and less of the image as the image gets bigger. An FFC block always sees the full image, no matter the size. Tests show LaMa with FFC works well from 256×256 up to 2048×2048, while normal convolution gets worse at higher sizes.

Even better: LaMa with FFC uses 27M parameters and beats normal LaMa versions with 45M and 74M parameters.

2. Better Loss Function

For large holes, many different fills could look correct. If you use a simple pixel loss (like L1), the model averages all possible answers and gives you a blurry result.

LaMa uses features from a segmentation network that understands scenes. This loss looks at the overall structure of the image, not just individual pixels, so the output is sharper and more natural.

3. Training with Large Masks

Most models train with small holes, so they learn to just copy nearby pixels. LaMa trains with large masks on purpose. When most of the image is missing, the model can't cheat — it has to actually understand the scene to fill it in.

How It's Built

  • Input: Image with holes + a mask showing where the holes are
  • Encoder: 3 blocks that shrink the image
  • Middle: 6-18 FFC blocks (the core that does the work)
  • Decoder: 3 blocks that bring it back to full size
  • Trained on: Places dataset and CelebA-HQ faces

What Works and What Doesn't

LaMa is great at filling in repeated patterns like fences, tiles, or textures. It also handles removing text from clothing well. But it can struggle when:

  • The hole covers a complex area with many different objects
  • The mask doesn't clearly cover the whole object you want to remove

Summary

LaMa uses Fourier transforms to give the network a global view of the image, trains with large masks to force real understanding, and uses a smarter loss function to avoid blurry results. It works at any resolution and uses fewer parameters than older methods.
